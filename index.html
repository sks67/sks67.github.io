Research

The Supernova Delay-Time Distribution in the Local Group

There are many open questions about the origin of supernovae: Are Type Ia supernova produced by white dwarfs accreting mass from a companion star, or a merger/collision with another white dwarf? Is the Chandrasekhar limit necessary for a white dwarf to explode as Type Ia? Do all massive stars produce a visible, core-collapse supernova? What is the cutoff mass for producing black holes/neutron stars? Not only are these questions missing pieces in our theory of stellar evolution, but they also affect areas of research where supernovae play a key role, such as measuring parameters of cosmology and dark energy, regulation of star-formation, chemical enrichment in galaxies, the formation of the interstellar medium, galactic inflows and outflows, and the acceleration of cosmic rays.

A powerful observational test for supernova progenitor models is the delay-time distribution (DTD), the hypothetical SN rate versus time since a delta-function burst of star-formation, making it similar in nature to the transfer function in signal processing. The DTD is the observed timescale on which SN progenitors, once formed, produce a supernova. Since any theoretical progenitor model can predict a timescale, the DTD is a direct constraint of that model.
